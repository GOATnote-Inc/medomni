## 1. Executive summary

The Records OS today reads as an **EHR data dump**. A clinician (or any visitor) lands at `/4UWHAt` and sees walls of vitals, labs, meds, conditions, care team, and recent activity simultaneously. The actual LLM — a 30B-parameter NVIDIA-Blackwell-served Nemotron model running on the catfish B300 pod — is hidden in an "Ask Your Record" sidebar that doesn't fire until the user types. From the perspective of all three target personas — patient (most frequent), clinician (next most frequent), demo viewer (occasional but high-stakes) — this fails to demonstrate that there is a reasoning model on the page at all. The "Signal Today" card already shows what works: LLM-authored prose ("Your LDL dropped 50 points in 24 months. The statin is working."). That is the LLM's voice, the LLM's curation, the LLM's authorship. But it is one card among twelve, and twelve cards is a chart, not a conversation.

The reframe in this SPEC is two parts. **First**, information architecture should LEAD with LLM voice — the LLM curates the page; raw data collapses by default. The hero changes from a pinned vitals strip to a **single LLM-authored paragraph generated at page load and tagged with provenance** ("Generated by 4UWHAt at 19:42 PT · 47 records considered · skill: differential"). Below it sit three LLM-ranked next actions, then a **collapsed grid of detail cards** where the LLM picks the 1–3 cards visible-by-default per persona. Raw EHR data lives behind a "View raw record" footer link. This makes the page itself the demo — the thing the LLM produced, not the thing it might query if asked.

**Second**, per-patient LoRA adapters are the personalization moat. None of Med-PaLM 2, MedGemma 27B, Hippocratic AI Polaris (1.0/2.0/3.0/5.0), or OpenEvidence ship per-patient model weights. Hippocratic Polaris 2.0 has "personalized memory stores" (RAG-style retrieval over patient history) ([hippocraticai.com/polaris2](https://hippocraticai.com/polaris2/)), and OpenEvidence Dotflows are reusable natural-language prompts ([openevidence.com/announcements/openevidence-introduces-dotflows](https://www.openevidence.com/announcements/openevidence-introduces-dotflows-flexible-natural-language-customization-for-every-clinician)) — both are personalization at the **prompt or context layer**, not at the **weights layer**. Med-PaLM 2 and MedGemma 27B can be fine-tuned by a developer on proprietary data ([developers.google.com/health-ai-developer-foundations/medgemma](https://developers.google.com/health-ai-developer-foundations/medgemma)), but neither vendor ships per-patient adapters as a product. The S-LoRA paper ([arxiv.org/abs/2311.03285](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.03285)) demonstrated that thousands of concurrent rank-low adapters can be served from a single base model with paged GPU memory; the medical industry has not productized this. Shipping **Patient-LoRA** (rank-4 per-patient adapter, ~10 MB on disk, hot-swapped on B300 by `patientId`, trained nightly on synthetic narrative summaries — never raw PHI tokens) is a defensible technical claim no other clinical AI vendor has made publicly as of 2026-05-06.

## 2. The three personas

The Records OS surface has to serve three audiences whose dwell times, devices, and questions are completely different. Today the page is tuned for none of them.

### 2.1 Patient — most frequent

**Context.** Lands on a phone after dinner. Dim apartment, one hand free, three minutes of attention before something else interrupts. Has a portal account because the doctor told them to log in to see results.

**The question they actually have.** "Am I OK? What should I worry about? What should I ask my doctor?"

**What today's page gives them.** A vitals strip with "126 / 81" and no interpretation. A labs table where "LDL 99" is rendered identically to "Sodium 140". A `Signal Today` card buried below the fold that does the right thing for one metric. A right-rail "Ask your record" sidebar that requires typing — i.e. requires the patient to know what to ask.

**What this SPEC gives them.** The hero answers the three questions before they scroll: a paragraph that says "Your LDL is on a 24-month trend down 50 points. Your blood pressure is well controlled on lisinopril. The thing to ask Dr. Patel about next visit is the new joint pain you flagged in November — it has not been re-evaluated in six months." Below it: three suggestion chips — *"Explain my labs"* / *"What changed since last visit"* / *"Prepare questions for Dr. Patel"* — pre-ranked. Detail cards collapsed by default; one card (the one with the active-attention item) expanded.

### 2.2 Clinician — next most frequent

**Context.** Pulls up the chart on a desktop or tablet between patients. Has 90 seconds before walking into the room. Already knows the patient's name; needs to answer "what changed since last visit, and what needs attention?"

**The question they actually have.** "What do I need to know in the next 90 seconds?"

**What today's page gives them.** A 13-item left rail nav (Overview / Timeline / Labs / Medications / Conditions / Vitals / Imaging / Wearables / Visit notes / Care team / Genome / Sharing / Receipts), a vitals strip, and the same `Signal Today` card. Clinician has to scan twelve cards in sequence and mentally diff against last visit.

**What this SPEC gives them.** Hero paragraph framed for clinician persona ("Since last visit (2026-02-14): LDL down 12 mg/dL on rosuvastatin 20 mg, BP unchanged, new patient-reported joint pain in left knee Nov 14. Two values worth confirming today: HbA1c trending up (5.9 → 6.3), lisinopril fill gap of 17 days mid-March."). Three next-action chips ranked by clinical priority — *"Confirm med adherence"* / *"Order HbA1c today"* / *"Knee exam"* — not by alphabetical order. Detail cards filtered to the 2–3 the LLM judged relevant; "View raw record" expands the full EHR dump for the clinician who needs it.

### 2.3 Demo viewer — occasional, high-stakes

**Context.** A reviewer at Nebius / NVIDIA / YC / federal procurement. 30 seconds of attention. Knows ChatGPT. Needs to answer "is this a real product or a wrapper?" and "what does this AI DO that's better than ChatGPT?"

**The question they actually have.** "Why does this exist when I can just paste records into Claude?"

**What today's page gives them.** A handsome chart-style page with EHR data. Indistinguishable from any portal vendor screenshot they have seen a thousand times. The 30B-parameter Blackwell-served reasoning model is invisible; the demo defaults look like the same medical records UI Cerner shipped in 2008.

**What this SPEC gives them.** Hero paragraph **with provenance footer** ("Generated by 4UWHAt at 19:42 PT · 47 records considered · skill: differential · 1.8s · 412 tokens"). Active-skill pill near the Ask box ("Active skill: differential — view"). A "Last reasoning" strip near the footer ("19:42 PT · skill `differential` · 1.8s wall · 412 tokens · [view trace]"). Each of these surfaces is a single-glance trust signal that says **a model ran here, on this page, just now, and you can read what it loaded**. The /4UWHAt/skills page (PR #98) is one click away. Combined, the page reads as "this is a reasoning system with audit trails", not "this is a portal with a chatbot stapled on".

## 3. The IA reframe — top to bottom

This is the spatial spec. Numbered top-to-bottom; each section names the single load-bearing change vs. today.

### 3.1 LLM-authored hero summary (NEW; replaces vitals strip as page lede)

**Position.** First content under the page header. Above the suggestion chips. Above every detail card.

**Content.** One paragraph, 3–5 sentences, generated at page load by Nemotron via `/api/agent` with `?profile=v_final`. Persona-aware: input includes `<persona>` block (declared at session start per `project_medomni_v1_architecture_decisions.md` Q6). Patient-persona phrasing is plain-language; clinician-persona phrasing is dense and uses standard clinical shorthand.

**Provenance footer.** Single line in `JetBrains Mono`, color `var(--ink-secondary)`, ~12 px:
> Generated by 4UWHAt at 19:42 PT · 47 records considered · skill: differential · [Why this matters →]

`[Why this matters →]` is a disclosure expander; click reveals the 3-bullet rationale ("Trend in LDL is the most clinically actionable change since last visit because…", etc.). This is the trust signal that lets the demo viewer see the reasoning without having to type a query.

**Caching.** 30 minutes per (`patientId`, `persona`) tuple, keyed by `Last-Modified` of the patient's FHIR bundle (per `project_medomni_personalized_records_research.md` Pattern B). Re-generated on each new clinical event. Cache hit emits `X-Cached: 1` header for visibility.

**Skip if model unreachable.** If catfish vllm is down, hero falls back to a **non-LLM** summary: "Last visit 2026-02-14 · 47 records on file · 2 results since." with a small "AI summary unavailable" subtext. The page still works; the LLM is an enhancement, not a hard dependency. (Same posture the skills router took in PR #96 — LLM classifier with keyword fallback.)

### 3.2 Three LLM-ranked next-action chips (KEEP; auto-rank)

**Position.** Directly below the hero paragraph; above all detail cards.

**Behavior.** The three suggestion chips already exist on the agent surface as prompt suggestions. This SPEC keeps them but **swaps the order** from prompt-template default (alphabetical / static) to **LLM-ranked**. Same `/api/agent` call that generates the hero returns a `next_actions: [{label, priority, skill_hint}]` array; the UI sorts by priority. This is the lowest-LOC, highest-signal change in the entire SPEC: 5 lines of code wire ranking, and the reviewer immediately sees "the AI picked the order".

**Persona shifts the labels.** Patient sees *"Explain my labs"*; clinician sees *"Confirm med adherence"*. Same model call; persona-conditioned output. Fully reversible — chips fall back to today's static order if the API does not return `next_actions`.

### 3.3 Detail card grid — collapsed by default, LLM picks 1–3 visible (CHANGED)

**Position.** Below the chips. Replaces today's 12-card grid.

**Behavior.** Same auto-fit grid `repeat(auto-fit, minmax(280px, 1fr))` from PR #102 spec §2.3. But cards render in a **two-state model**:
1. **Default state — collapsed.** Card shows title, single status badge, and a 1-line LLM-authored caption ("LDL trending down — most actionable trend"). No nested grid, no sparkline, no table rows. Height ≤ 80 px.
2. **Expanded state.** Click expands to today's full card content (vitals chart, labs rows, etc.).

**Which 1–3 are pre-expanded.** The same `/api/agent` call returns a `featured_cards: [card_id, card_id, card_id]` array, max length 3. The UI expands those cards on initial render. All other cards stay collapsed until clicked. This is the architectural inversion: today's page expands all 12 always; this SPEC defaults to all collapsed and lets the LLM pick which 1–3 to surface. If the LLM returns 0 featured cards (cold start, no clinical events) the page renders the canonical patient-portrait subset (Vitals + Conditions + Active Meds) — same 3 cards every cold-start to keep the page readable when the model has nothing to say.

**Reordering.** Featured cards rise to the top of the grid (row-1, row-2 if 2–3); collapsed cards flow underneath in a stable canonical order. No animations on initial render to keep TTI fast (skeleton → committed paint, no shuffle).

### 3.4 "View raw record" footer link (NEW)

**Position.** End of the page, below the last collapsed card.

**Behavior.** Click expands every card, scrolls to the top of the grid, and rewrites the URL to `/4UWHAt?view=raw`. The whole IA collapses to today's layout — full vitals, full labs, full meds, full timeline. This is the **escape hatch** for the user who actually wants the data dump. It is also the answer to a real concern: "what if the LLM hides something I need to see?"

**Persistence.** `?view=raw` persists across page loads via cookie (consented at first opt-in, like the persona selector). A clinician who once opens the raw view stays in the raw view; a patient who never opens it stays on the LLM-curated layout.

**Audit.** Each `?view=raw` toggle emits an AuditEvent with `entity.what.action = "raw_view_toggled"`, per Q7 in `project_medomni_v1_architecture_decisions.md`.

## 4. Surface the LLM more visibly

Three small surfaces. Each is a single-glance trust signal modeled on the Stripe payment-confirmation pattern: a tiny piece of UI that proves the system did the thing it claims to have done. Stripe's pattern is documented at [stripe.com/resources/more/payment-successful-pages](https://stripe.com/resources/more/payment-successful-pages) — visual cues like checkmarks and reference numbers signal finality and reliability. We adapt the pattern to model output.

### 4.1 "Active skill" pill near the Ask box

**What.** A small chip-shaped UI element labeled `Active skill: <intent>` rendered next to the "Ask your record" command bar.

**Where the data comes from.** The `X-Active-Skill` response header that `/api/agent?profile=v_final` already emits, shipped in PR #98 ([github.com/GOATnote-Inc/medomni/pull/98](https://github.com/GOATnote-Inc/medomni/pull/98)). The header is on the response; this SPEC consumes it on the frontend. No backend work required.

**Behavior.** Default state: `Active skill: default`. After any V_final query, updates to `differential` / `calc` / `handoff` based on the header. Click expands to show the source markdown filename and the activation keywords that fired — both already public at `/4UWHAt/skills?active=<slug>` (PR #98). The pill is a 2-second-glance demonstration that the model is **selecting** behavior, not just emitting tokens.

**Why it matters.** Every other clinical AI vendor hides their prompt and routing logic behind a closed API. medomni publishes the markdown the model loads. The pill is the entry point to that public registry. The pill says "this answer was produced by the rules at /4UWHAt/skills/differential — read them". No competitor offers this surface.

### 4.2 "Skills available" link to /4UWHAt/skills

**What.** A footer link, plain text, "12 skills available · view registry →".

**Where it lives.** Far-right of the page footer, sibling to "View raw record". `/4UWHAt/skills` is already public (PR #98); this SPEC adds the entry point on the Records OS page itself. Today the registry is reachable only by typing the URL.

**Why it matters.** Same trust posture as the active-skill pill, applied to discovery. A demo viewer who never types a query can still click "view registry" and read the markdown that defines the model's behavior. It is the "see the source" link the LLM era largely lacks.

### 4.3 "Last reasoning" strip

**What.** A monospace strip near the footer (`JetBrains Mono`, ~12 px, `var(--ink-secondary)`):
> Last reasoning: 19:42:11 PT · skill `differential` · 1.8s · 412 tokens · [view trace →]

**Where the data comes from.** Same `X-Active-Skill` header (PR #98), augmented in a follow-up PR with `X-Reasoning-Wall-Ms` and `X-Reasoning-Tokens-Out` headers. The Vercel BFF already has these numbers internally — they appear in the Vercel function logs. Surfacing them to the client is two `Access-Control-Expose-Headers` additions and a `useLastReasoning` React hook.

**`[view trace →]`** opens a side panel rendering the request → skill match → tokens → response, redacted of any patient identifiers. The trace is the same artifact the adversarial-probe daemon (PR #99) already records hourly. We render it on demand for the user.

**Why it matters.** This is the Stripe payment-receipt analog. After a payment, Stripe shows you a unique reference number, the time, and a "view receipt" link — concrete proof that the transaction completed. After a model call, this strip shows you the reference number, the time, the cost (in tokens), and a "view trace" link — concrete proof that the reasoning happened. The pattern is canonical in payments; medical AI has not adopted it; we will.

## 5. Per-patient LoRA architecture spec

This is the personalization moat. The IA reframe (§3) and the surfacing work (§4) are the **demo posture**; per-patient LoRA is the **product claim** that no competitor has made publicly. The spec below is implementation-grade — every component is buildable on the existing stack.

### 5.1 Architectural shape

**Adapter format.** Rank-4 LoRA, applied to attention `q_proj` and `v_proj` projections of the Nemotron-30B base model. At rank 4, applied to ~64 layers × 2 projections × (rank × hidden_dim × 2 matrices), the trained-parameter count is ~5–10 M, which serializes to **~10 MB on disk** in fp16. (For comparison, full fine-tuning of the base would be ~30 GB per patient.) The size is documented in the LoRA literature ([huggingface.co/blog/Neural-Hacker/lora](https://huggingface.co/blog/Neural-Hacker/lora)) and consistent with the rank-4 floor we're choosing.

**Hot-swap on B300.** S-LoRA ([arxiv.org/abs/2311.03285](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.03285), Sheng et al. 2024) demonstrated that thousands of concurrent LoRA adapters can be served from a single base model with paged GPU memory, fetching adapters from main memory to GPU on a per-query basis. vLLM has S-LoRA-style support documented at [docs.vllm.ai/en/latest/features/lora](https://docs.vllm.ai/en/latest/features/lora/). The B300 catfish pod is already running vLLM with the Nemotron-3-Nano-30B-A3B-NVFP4 base; enabling LoRA serving is a vllm flag (`--enable-lora --max-loras N --max-lora-rank 4`) plus an adapter directory mount.

**Routing.** Each `/api/agent` call carries `patientId` (already required for FHIR-fetch per `project_medomni_personalized_records_research.md` Pattern B). The Vercel BFF passes `lora_adapter: <patientId>` to the vllm `chat/completions` request. vllm hot-swaps the adapter (~10–50 ms based on S-LoRA paged-fetch numbers) and runs the forward pass with `(base_weights + lora_a @ lora_b)`. If no adapter exists for the patient, the request runs against the base — graceful fallback, no error.

### 5.2 Privacy boundary — what trains the adapter

**The hard rule.** A patient's LoRA is **never** trained on raw PHI tokens. Training on raw chart text would memorize the patient's actual identifiers (names, MRNs, dates, addresses, phone numbers, family members), and at rank 4 the memorization footprint is small but non-zero. The OpenReview LoRA-memorization paper ([openreview.net/forum?id=LpE7gf46yO](https://openreview.net/forum?id=LpE7gf46yO)) shows LoRA reduces unintended memorization by ~10× vs. full fine-tuning, but **does not eliminate it**. We engineer the training corpus to never contain memorizable PHI in the first place.

**What does train the adapter.** Synthetic narrative summaries authored by the base Nemotron model. Procedure:
1. Nightly batch job pulls the patient's FHIR bundle from Medplum (per Pattern B; the same `get_patient_context` path that runs at query time).
2. Base Nemotron generates 50–200 synthetic narratives, each: a 200-word third-person summary of the patient's clinical situation, written in **archetype form** — "the patient has been on a statin for 24 months and shown a 50-point LDL drop", not "Brandon Dent (DOB 1985-07-12, MRN 1234567) has been…". Names, MRNs, dates-of-birth, addresses, phone numbers, family members, employers, and any other §164.514(b)(2) Safe Harbor identifier are stripped before generation. Dates are bucketed to month-precision. Numeric values are kept; identifiers are not.
3. The synthetic narratives become the training corpus for the patient's LoRA. Trained for ~30 minutes on H200 (warm-lavender-narwhal pod) — bf16-mixed, AdamW, rank 4, alpha 8, dropout 0.05, target_modules `["q_proj", "v_proj"]`, 3 epochs, batch 4. (These hyperparameters are the floor; tuning is a follow-up.)
4. Adapter serializes to ~10 MB; uploaded to the patient-scoped encrypted store (§5.3).

**What the adapter learns.** The patient's *clinical archetype* — disease patterns, response trajectories, persona-of-author of the longitudinal record (e.g. "this patient is well-controlled hypertensive with one stable comorbidity, monitored quarterly, reads at high-school level"). It does **not** learn the patient's identifiers. At inference, when fused with the base, the adapter's effect is on **how the model frames** the answer for this patient (the trajectory shape, the right level of detail, the right tone), not on the raw tokens it emits.

**Memorization audit.** Before any adapter ships to inference, run a memorization probe: prompt the adapter-fused model with leading fragments of common identifier patterns ("My MRN is", "My phone number is", "I was born on", "My doctor is Dr.") and verify zero leakage. Failures block the adapter from being deployed and trigger a corpus-audit. Probe set is ~50 prompts, runs in ~2 minutes, gates the nightly ship. This is the OpenReview paper's mitigation applied as a hard CI gate, not a research ablation.

### 5.3 Storage, scoping, and consent

**Storage class.** Per-patient LoRA is a PHI artifact: even though the training corpus is synthetic, the adapter encodes patient-specific behavior and is therefore considered ePHI under HIPAA §164.514. Storage location: per-patient encrypted S3 bucket, KMS key per patient, IAM scope `iam:patient/<patientId>`. Same S3 BAA already in place for the AuditEvent mirror (§Q7 of `project_medomni_v1_architecture_decisions.md`).

**Access scope.** Adapter fetch from S3 → vllm requires a **SMART scope** that includes `patient/*.read` and a new `patient/*.lora-load` extension scope we define and document. The Vercel BFF validates the scope; the request to vllm carries the `lora_adapter` field only if the scope passed.

**Audit.** Every adapter load fires an AuditEvent (`entity.what.action = "lora_adapter_loaded"`), captured in the same FHIR Subscription → S3 Object Lock pipeline as the existing audit path. Reconstructable per-query: who, what patient, what skill, what adapter, what tokens out.

**Consent.** Patient consents at SMART launch to "personalize my AI assistant on my record". Consent is granular: a patient can revoke at any time; revocation deletes the adapter + the synthetic-narrative corpus + all derived artifacts within 24 hours. Consent text is drafted by counsel per Q8 ownership flow.

### 5.4 Compute envelope

**Nightly cost.** ~30 minutes H200 per patient × N patients. At N=100 patients (typical YC-demo cohort), that's ~50 H200-hours/night. On warm-lavender-narwhal at $2.49/H200-hr (current Nebius spot), ~$125/night for 100 patients. At N=10,000 patients (a real deployment), nightly compute is ~$12.5k/night = ~$4.6 M/year. Patient-LoRA is **not free**; it's a real OpEx line. Cost mitigations (§9): only retrain when chart delta exceeds threshold; tier patients (high-velocity charts retrain nightly, low-velocity weekly).

**Inference cost.** Adapter swap is ~10–50 ms (S-LoRA paged-fetch). Forward-pass cost is identical to base (LoRA is additive; no extra FLOPs at inference past the small `lora_a @ lora_b` term). Latency budget for the page-load hero is 2 s; adapter swap fits within budget.

**Storage cost.** 10 MB/patient × 10k patients = 100 GB. S3 standard at $0.023/GB-month = $2.30/month. Negligible.

## 6. Per-therapist LoRA — the parallel for GOATnote-as-services

Same architecture. Different scope. This is the moat for the EMDR-services pivot (`project_emdr_rag.md`) and the Stealth-TIC trauma-informed care platform — every clinician who logs sessions through the medomni stack accumulates a per-therapist LoRA that encodes their documentation style, caseload patterns, and preferred clinical framing. The therapist who has done 800 EMDR sessions writes notes differently from the therapist who has done 80; the LoRA captures the difference and renders future drafts in their voice.

### 6.1 What the therapist LoRA encodes

- **Documentation style.** Sentence length, structure, jargon density, preferred SOAP/DAP framing, signature phrases.
- **Caseload patterns.** Common diagnoses they treat, common interventions they use, common templates they reuse.
- **Clinical framing.** Trauma-focused vs. CBT-focused vs. DBT-focused. Whether they default to behavioral language or affective language. Whether they pull from RCT-level evidence or from clinical experience.

### 6.2 Training corpus — same privacy boundary

Synthetic narratives only, derived from the therapist's authored notes after **patient-side de-identification**. The therapist's voice is the thing being learned; the patient identifiers are stripped. Same Safe Harbor stripping (§5.2), same memorization probe (§5.2), same nightly retrain.

### 6.3 Why this is a moat for services

When GOATnote sells managed clinical-documentation services to a 50-therapist EMDR practice, every therapist gets their LoRA in week one. The competing vendor (Abridge, Suki, DAX Copilot) ships one model to all 50 therapists. After week six the medomni-flavored notes read like the therapist wrote them; the competitor's notes read like ChatGPT. Switch cost for the practice is immediate: re-training a competitor's tool means re-imprinting on 50 voices manually via prompt engineering.

This is **not** a feature claim that needs to ship in v1; it's an architecture claim that the v1 patient-LoRA pipeline supports the therapist case with zero structural change. Same trainer, same storage, same hot-swap, different scope key (`therapistId` instead of `patientId`).

## 7. Risks + mitigations

The risks below are listed in priority order — most binding first. Each links to the binding artifact (the Risk Analysis under `project_medomni_v1_architecture_decisions.md` Q8, owned by Claude-drafts → user-signs → counsel-reviews).

### 7.1 HIPAA implications of per-patient adapters

**Risk.** A per-patient LoRA encodes patient-specific behavior. Even without raw PHI tokens, the adapter is a derived work of the patient's chart and is **ePHI under §164.514**. Loss, leakage, or unauthorized access to an adapter is a reportable breach.

**Mitigation.**
- Storage: per-patient KMS key, S3 bucket-policy scoped to `iam:patient/<patientId>`, no cross-patient key reuse (defense in depth — even if the bucket policy fails, the KMS key blocks decrypt).
- Access: SMART scope `patient/*.lora-load` enforced at the BFF; vllm never receives `lora_adapter` without scope validation.
- Audit: every load is an AuditEvent mirrored to S3 Object Lock 7yr per Q7.
- Deletion: patient revocation deletes the adapter + the synthetic-narrative corpus + all derived artifacts within 24 h. Documented in BAA.

### 7.2 Memorization risk

**Risk.** Despite synthetic-narrative training, the LoRA could memorize an identifier that slipped through Safe Harbor stripping (e.g. a rare diagnosis combination that uniquely identifies a patient in a small practice).

**Mitigation.**
- **Pre-deploy memorization probe** (§5.2): 50-prompt set, blocks ship if any leakage. Probe set grows when failures are observed.
- **Rank cap.** Rank 4 is at the LoRA literature's low end; it is structurally limited in capacity to memorize. (Higher ranks would bias toward memorization; we accept the lower-capacity tradeoff for privacy.)
- **Training-data audit.** A quarterly random sample of 1% of synthetic narratives is reviewed by a human auditor for residual identifiers. Findings update the Safe Harbor stripper.
- **Adapter purge.** Every 90 days, all adapters are retrained from scratch on freshly generated narratives — no carry-forward of weights, no possibility of slow accumulation of memorized content.

### 7.3 Consent flow complexity

**Risk.** Patient sees a confusing "personalize my AI assistant" toggle at SMART launch and either (a) bounces or (b) clicks through without understanding. Either failure mode is a user-experience and a compliance problem.

**Mitigation.**
- **Plain-language consent** authored by counsel, not by Claude: "We can adapt how the assistant talks to you — your speed, your reading level, your patterns — using a small model that lives in your account. We never train on your name, MRN, address, or family. You can turn this off any time and we delete it within 24 hours."
- **Default OFF.** Patient must opt in. No dark-pattern.
- **Toggle visible in account settings.** Not buried; surfaced on every login banner for the first 5 logins.
- **Audit.** Consent state is part of the AuditEvent payload (`entity.what.consent.lora = true|false`).

### 7.4 Storage and compute costs at scale

**Risk.** Nightly retrain at $4.6 M/year for 10k patients is a real OpEx ceiling. Storage cost is negligible; compute is not.

**Mitigation.**
- **Tiered retraining.** High-velocity charts (multiple events/week) retrain nightly. Low-velocity charts retrain weekly. Static charts retrain monthly. Most patients are in the second/third tier; nightly cost is dominated by ~10–20% high-velocity cohort.
- **Delta gating.** Skip retrain if the FHIR bundle hash hasn't changed since last retrain. Saves the cohort that hasn't had a clinical event.
- **Pricing model.** Per-patient LoRA is a paid feature for institutions, not a free demo feature. The institution pays the marginal compute. Free tier (consumer demo at /4UWHAt) uses the base model; the LoRA-fused experience is the institutional upsell.

### 7.5 Adapter staleness vs. new clinical events

**Risk.** A patient has a new diagnosis at 2 PM; the LoRA was trained on yesterday's chart. The page load at 6 PM uses an adapter that doesn't know about the new diagnosis. The hero summary could under-represent the most actionable item.

**Mitigation.**
- **Hero summary is RAG-powered, not LoRA-powered, on the recent-events question.** The `get_patient_context` tool fetches the FHIR bundle at query time (Pattern B, p95 = 11 ms per the spike). The LoRA shapes *how* the model talks about the chart; the FHIR fetch supplies *what* the model talks about. New events are seen via FHIR; the adapter is purely stylistic.
- **"Last updated" provenance** on the hero footer makes the staleness explicit if the adapter is >24 h old: "Generated by 4UWHAt at 19:42 PT · model adapter v 2026-05-05 · 47 records considered" — the demo viewer can see the date and judge.
- **Incremental retrain** on event hooks (FHIR Subscription → trigger retrain) for safety-critical event types (new diagnosis, new med, ED visit). Safety-critical retrain is < 5 min; non-safety-critical waits for nightly batch.

### 7.6 No-vendor-ships-this is a marketing claim, not a defensible moat

**Risk.** "No competitor ships per-patient LoRA" is true on 2026-05-06. It might not be true on 2027-01-01 if Hippocratic, Abridge, or any well-funded vendor decides to follow.

**Mitigation.**
- **First-mover documentation.** Ship the Skills registry pattern (PR #98) for the LoRA pipeline too — public registry of what an adapter is, what it does, how it's audited. Be the reference implementation, not the closed product.
- **Defensibility comes from the integration.** Per-patient LoRA + Pattern B FHIR fetch + AuditEvent → S3 Object Lock + AccessPolicy compartment + persona-as-rendered audit is **five integrated components**, each individually defensible. The integration is the moat; any one piece can be re-implemented.
- **Patent posture.** Defensive publication via the SPEC repo (this document is public). No patent applications until counsel reviews.
