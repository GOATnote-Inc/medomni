# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-TDAP-PREGNANCY

It does sound like a lot, and the question makes sense — but there's a specific reason the recommendation is per-pregnancy rather than per-person.

**Why every pregnancy.** The ACIP and ACOG recommendation is to give Tdap during *every* pregnancy, not just once per adult lifetime. The reason isn't that prior Tdap stopped working for you — it's about your baby. When you get Tdap during pregnancy, your immune system makes pertussis (whooping cough) antibodies, and those antibodies cross the placenta to your baby. The level of protection in the newborn depends on the antibody you generate during *this* pregnancy, which fades over time after vaccination. A Tdap from two years ago doesn't transfer enough antibody to this baby. So the recommendation is essentially "fresh dose each pregnancy."

**Timing.** The sweet spot is between 27 and 36 weeks of gestation, with earlier in that window generally preferred to give maximum time for antibody production and transfer. At 28 weeks, you're right in that window — this is a great time to do it.

**Why this matters.** Pertussis is most dangerous in very young infants. Babies under about 2 months of age can't be effectively vaccinated themselves yet, and pertussis at that age can cause apnea (stopping breathing), pneumonia, hospitalization, and rarely death. Most infant pertussis deaths in the US are in babies under 3 months. Maternal Tdap is what closes that gap — it gives your newborn a borrowed layer of protection during exactly the window when she's most vulnerable.

**Cocooning — the family piece.** Maternal Tdap is the centerpiece, but it's even better if the people who'll be holding the baby are also up to date. Your partner, the baby's grandparents, older siblings, regular caregivers — anyone who'll have close contact in those first months should make sure their Tdap booster is current (within the last 10 years for routine adult Td/Tdap, with at least one dose of Tdap in adulthood). This is called "cocooning."

**Postpartum Tdap is not the same.** If maternal Tdap is missed during pregnancy, getting it postpartum is better than not at all, but it's a fallback — it doesn't give the baby antibodies before she's born, which is the whole point. Don't aim for postpartum on purpose.

**Safety.** Tdap during pregnancy has been studied extensively — large pharmacovigilance datasets, randomized trials, and observational cohorts. The safety profile in pregnancy is reassuring; there's no signal for adverse pregnancy or newborn outcomes attributable to maternal Tdap. The most common side effect is a sore arm at the injection site, sometimes a low-grade fever or fatigue for a day, which resolves on its own.

**What this doesn't replace.** Maternal Tdap doesn't substitute for your baby's DTaP series — once she's born, she'll still get DTaP at 2, 4, 6, 15-18 months, and again at 4-6 years per the routine schedule. The maternal dose just bridges the time before her own immunity kicks in.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest going ahead with the Tdap your OB recommended. Each pregnancy gets its own dose, and 28 weeks is the right time. Use the appointment to ask about your partner's and any older child's Tdap status too.
