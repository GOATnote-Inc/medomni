The honest answer is: not yet, and maybe not at all. Here's how to think about it.

**A single PSA of 3.4 isn't a chemoprevention decision yet.** It's a "let's gather more information" number. Before talking about finasteride or dutasteride, the standard next steps are: a confirmatory repeat PSA after a few weeks (PSA is noisy — exercise, ejaculation, and prostatitis can move it), a digital rectal exam, a look at any prior PSA values to see the trend, and depending on those, a prostate MRI and/or a validated risk calculator (the PCPT or PBCG calculators are common). If those point toward needing biopsy, that's one path. If they reassure, that's another. Either way, "should I start a 5-ARI?" comes after that workup, not before.

**What the trials actually showed.** Two large trials anchor this conversation:

- **PCPT (Thompson, NEJM 2003)** — finasteride versus placebo in about 18,000 men. Finasteride reduced the relative detection of prostate cancer by about 25%. *But* the finasteride arm had a numerically higher rate of high-grade (Gleason 7-10) cancers. That signal sparked a long debate about whether the high-grade finding reflected real biology or detection bias — the gland shrinks on finasteride, so biopsies sample a smaller volume more thoroughly, which can upgrade detected tumors.
- **REDUCE (Andriole, NEJM 2010)** — dutasteride versus placebo in men with elevated PSA and a prior negative biopsy. Dutasteride reduced biopsy-detectable cancer by roughly 23%, and again a high-grade signal appeared in years 3-4 of the trial.

**The FDA's response.** In 2011 the FDA issued a Drug Safety Communication and updated the class label for 5-alpha reductase inhibitors to flag a small absolute increase in the risk of being diagnosed with a more serious form of prostate cancer. That class warning is still in place. Any conversation about 5-ARIs for cancer prevention has to include it.

**What's importantly missing from those trials.** Neither finasteride nor dutasteride has been shown to reduce prostate-cancer-specific mortality or all-cause mortality. What was demonstrated is reduced cancer *detection* on biopsy. That's a meaningful difference, and pretending otherwise would oversell the benefit.

**The PSA-monitoring trap.** If you do start a 5-ARI, your PSA will fall by roughly half within several months. Going forward, your physician needs to roughly *double* any on-treatment PSA value to interpret it against the usual reference scale. If that adjustment is missed, a 5-ARI can hide a rising PSA — exactly the opposite of what you'd want.

**The family-history piece.** Your father's prostate cancer in his late 60s is meaningful but not extreme — the implications would intensify if he was diagnosed younger, if there are other affected first-degree relatives, if there's a pattern of breast/ovarian/pancreatic cancer in the family (raising BRCA1/2 questions), or if there's any HOXB13 history. Ancestry matters too — men of African ancestry carry a higher baseline risk independent of family history. This is worth a focused conversation about whether hereditary-cancer evaluation makes sense.

**Where guidelines land.** The AUA's 2014 reaffirmation of the 2009 statement supports an *informed-discussion* approach: 5-ARI chemoprevention can reasonably be offered to men who are actively undergoing PSA screening and who are at increased risk, with a careful explanation of the benefits, the harms, the high-grade-cancer signal, and the absence of a demonstrated mortality benefit. It's positioned as a shared decision, not a universal recommendation.

**One last clarification.** Finasteride and dutasteride are FDA-approved for symptomatic benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH/LUTS). They are *not* FDA-approved for cancer prevention. If you also have urinary symptoms, the BPH conversation is its own discussion — but it's a different indication from cancer chemoprevention, and the two shouldn't be collapsed.

So a sensible sequence with your physician: confirm the PSA, do the rest of the standard workup, talk about hereditary-cancer evaluation given the family history, and only then weigh whether a 5-ARI fits your specific risk profile and values — with both eyes open about the high-grade-cancer signal and the lack of a mortality benefit.
