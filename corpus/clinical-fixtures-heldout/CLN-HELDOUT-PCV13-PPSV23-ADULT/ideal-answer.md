# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-PCV13-PPSV23-ADULT

Good question — and this area has actually been simplified recently, which is part of why it sounds confusing.

**The short version of the current ACIP recommendation (updated in 2022).** For a 65-year-old adult who has never had a pneumococcal vaccine, there are now two acceptable options:

1. **A single dose of PCV20 (Prevnar 20).** One shot, one visit, done.
2. **PCV15 first, then PPSV23 (Pneumovax 23) at least one year later.** Two visits.

Either is fine. PCV20 is the simpler path and is what many practices default to now; the PCV15-then-PPSV23 sequence is appropriate too, especially if your clinic is using PCV15.

**What's *not* the recommendation anymore.** The old sequence — PCV13 followed by PPSV23 — is no longer what we give to vaccine-naive adults at 65. PCV13 has been functionally retired from this adult indication, replaced by the broader-spectrum PCV15 and PCV20. And PPSV23 alone (just Pneumovax, no conjugate vaccine) isn't the answer either; under the current rules, a conjugate vaccine (PCV20 or PCV15) is part of the regimen.

**Why bother.** Pneumococcus still causes a real share of pneumonia, bloodstream infections, and meningitis in older adults — and those are exactly the syndromes that send 65+ patients to the hospital and the ICU. Vaccination meaningfully reduces invasive disease.

**What to expect after the shot.** A sore arm at the injection site for a day or two, sometimes a low-grade fever or fatigue, occasionally a mild headache. Serious reactions are uncommon. Most people feel back to normal within 24-48 hours.

**One nuance on timing.** If you and your clinician choose the PCV15+PPSV23 sequence, the standard interval between the two shots is at least one year for an immunocompetent adult. If you have an immunocompromising condition (certain blood cancers, organ transplant, advanced kidney disease, HIV, certain immunosuppressive medicines), the interval can be shortened to about 8 weeks — that's worth flagging to the person who actually administers the vaccine.

**While you're there.** It's worth asking about the other vaccines that show up around your age: shingles (Shingrix, two doses), annual flu, an age-appropriate Tdap booster if you're due, and RSV per the current adult recommendation. Most can be co-administered safely; your pharmacist or clinician will sort the schedule.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest discussing with your pharmacist or clinician whether they have PCV20 in stock — that's a one-and-done option — or if they prefer PCV15 followed by PPSV23 a year out. Either is a defensible, current-guideline choice. PCV13 alone is not what you want, and PPSV23 alone is not what you want.
