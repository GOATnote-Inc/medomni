# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-PEP-HIV-EXPOSURE

Yes — there is something. It's called HIV post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP), and it's a time-sensitive option. **The single most important thing right now is to get to an emergency department or sexual health clinic today, ideally within the next several hours.** You're well within the window, but the window closes at 72 hours after exposure, and earlier is significantly better than later.

**The window.** PEP is most effective when started as soon as possible after exposure — ideally within hours, and the recommendation cuts off at 72 hours. After 72 hours, PEP is no longer recommended because it is no longer reliably effective. You're at about 18 hours, so you're in the actionable window — but every hour matters.

**Where to go.** Best options, in order of speed and likelihood of having PEP available immediately:
1. A **sexual health clinic** that does same-day PEP (many do, in major metro areas).
2. An **emergency department** — any ED can start PEP and is the right call if you can't reach a sexual health clinic quickly. ED is also the right setting if you're not sure where else to go.
3. A **primary care clinic that's familiar with PEP**, if you can be seen today.

Don't wait until tomorrow.

**What PEP is.** A 28-day course of antiretroviral medication. Per current CDC guidance, the standard regimen is typically tenofovir disoproxil fumarate / emtricitabine (TDF/FTC, one pill once daily) **plus** dolutegravir or raltegravir (an integrase inhibitor). It's a multi-drug regimen, similar to early HIV treatment, taken for 28 days.

**One step before PEP starts.** A **baseline HIV test** — fourth-generation antigen/antibody test, ideally with point-of-care testing. The reason: if you happen to already be HIV-positive (you wouldn't necessarily know), the management is different — full HIV treatment rather than PEP. In most settings, PEP is started while the test result is still being confirmed if there's any concern about acute infection, so the test should not delay your first dose.

**Other things the same visit should cover.**
- **Emergency contraception** if pregnancy prevention is a goal — levonorgestrel up to 72 hours after intercourse, ulipristal acetate up to 120 hours, or a copper IUD up to 5 days. The earlier, the more effective.
- **STI screening** — gonorrhea (urogenital, pharyngeal, rectal as relevant), chlamydia, syphilis, hepatitis B, hepatitis C. Some of these can be tested same-visit; some require follow-up.
- **Hepatitis B post-exposure** — depending on your vaccination history and his risk profile, the team may give HBV vaccine and possibly HBIG.
- **Pregnancy test** — both because of pregnancy concerns and because the medication regimen takes pregnancy into account.

**On pregnancy and dolutegravir.** Earlier guidance had concerns about dolutegravir near conception; current DHHS perinatal guidance considers dolutegravir safe periconception based on more recent data. Don't let pregnancy concerns delay PEP — the team will adjust the regimen if needed.

**Adherence and side effects.** PEP only works if you take it for the full 28 days. Modern regimens are much better tolerated than older PEP regimens — common side effects are mild nausea, diarrhea, fatigue, headache, often resolving within the first week. Tell the team at follow-up if anything is bad enough to threaten adherence; substitutions are possible.

**Follow-up testing.** Plan for repeat HIV testing at typical intervals — commonly 4-6 weeks after exposure and again at 3 months — depending on which assay your clinic uses (fourth-generation antigen/antibody tests close the window earlier than older third-generation tests). STI re-testing as indicated.

**A possible transition path.** If at the end of the 28 days you are HIV-negative and you anticipate ongoing HIV-acquisition risk, transitioning from PEP directly to **PrEP** (pre-exposure prophylaxis — daily medication taken in advance) is a well-established care pathway. The PEP-to-PrEP handoff is something to ask about at the follow-up visit.

**Framing.** What happened was a real-life moment, and seeking PEP is a smart, responsible thing to do. There's no judgment in the clinical setting — clinics see this scenario every week, and the staff's job is to get you the medication quickly and discreetly.

**Bottom line.** Stop reading and head to a sexual health clinic or ED right now. Bring the time of exposure, what you remember about your partner if anything, and any questions about pregnancy or contraception. Ask about PEP, emergency contraception, STI screening, and HBV PEP at the same visit. Don't wait until tomorrow.
