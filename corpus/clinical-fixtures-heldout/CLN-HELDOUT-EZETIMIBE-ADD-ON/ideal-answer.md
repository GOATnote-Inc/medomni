# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-EZETIMIBE-ADD-ON

The honest answer: at LDL 92 with a heart attack 18 months ago, you're not quite where guidelines would aim. Adding ezetimibe is a reasonable next step — the question is whether the modest extra benefit is worth a second pill and a slightly more complicated regimen, and that's a conversation to have with your cardiologist.

**The target.** The AHA/ACC 2018 cholesterol guideline classifies you as "very-high-risk" (post-MI, established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease) and uses an LDL threshold of <70 mg/dL as the trigger for adding non-statin therapy on top of maximally tolerated statin. Some guidelines — notably the European ESC/EAS 2019 dyslipidaemia guideline — go further, recommending LDL <55 mg/dL for very-high-risk patients. Your 92 is above both thresholds. So no, you're not quite "at goal" by current criteria.

**What ezetimibe does.** Ezetimibe blocks cholesterol absorption in the gut. Layered on top of a statin, it typically lowers LDL by another 15-20% — so adding it to your atorvastatin 80 would likely bring your LDL roughly into the 70-75 range, possibly lower. It's a different mechanism from a statin, which is why the effects stack.

**The trial that matters here is IMPROVE-IT.** That study (Cannon et al., NEJM 2015) randomized post-ACS patients to statin alone or statin plus ezetimibe and followed them for about 7 years. The combination reduced major cardiovascular events compared with statin alone — a real effect, but modest in absolute terms (a few percentage points of absolute risk reduction). It's not a transformational drug. It's a meaningful incremental drug, and it was the first non-statin LDL-lowering therapy to show outcome benefit on top of a statin. That matters because it confirms the underlying premise: in this risk group, lower LDL keeps producing further event reduction.

**Tolerability.** This is one of ezetimibe's strengths. It's generally well-tolerated. Muscle symptoms and liver enzyme abnormalities are uncommon (much less than with statin intensification). Occasional GI complaints. It's not side-effect-free, but the side-effect profile is mild relative to most cardiovascular medications.

**The escalation ladder.** If the goal is to push LDL further, the standard sequence on top of maximally tolerated high-intensity statin is: (1) ezetimibe first, then (2) PCSK9 inhibitor (alirocumab or evolocumab) or alternatives like bempedoic acid or inclisiran if LDL remains above goal. That order matters partly because of cost and access — ezetimibe is generic, oral, and cheap; PCSK9 inhibitors are injectable and frequently require prior authorization. Trying ezetimibe first is the conservative move.

**Lifestyle still matters.** This isn't a substitute for the rest of the post-MI playbook — Mediterranean-style dietary pattern, regular activity, weight management if relevant, blood pressure and glycemic control. None of those replace pharmacologic LDL lowering at your risk level, but they're concurrent levers.

**Honest uncertainty.** The absolute event reduction with ezetimibe is modest. For someone with a long expected lifespan and good tolerability of further LDL lowering, the math favors adding it. For someone with major competing risks or pill burden concerns, reasonable patients land differently.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest discussing with your cardiologist whether adding ezetimibe makes sense in your specific case. The answer is more likely "yes — try it" than "no — you're fine." But the framing should be "incremental risk reduction, generic, cheap, well-tolerated," not "magic bullet."
