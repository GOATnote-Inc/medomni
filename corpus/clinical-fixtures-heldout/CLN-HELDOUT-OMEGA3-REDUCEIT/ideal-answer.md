# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-OMEGA3-REDUCEIT

Short answer: no, they are not the same thing — and the difference actually matters for someone in your situation. Your neighbor's fish oil is a supplement; icosapent ethyl is a prescription drug with cardiovascular outcome data in the population you fit.

**The two trials that explain why "fish oil" is not one thing.**

REDUCE-IT (Bhatt et al., NEJM 2019) studied prescription icosapent ethyl — pure EPA, 4 g/day — in over 8,000 statin-treated patients with elevated triglycerides who either had established cardiovascular disease or had diabetes plus additional risk factors. After about 5 years, icosapent ethyl reduced major cardiovascular events by roughly 25% relative to placebo. That is a real, clinically meaningful effect.

STRENGTH (Nicholls et al., JAMA 2020) was a similarly-designed trial that used a different omega-3 formulation — omega-3 carboxylic acids, an EPA + DHA mixture — at a similar dose. It did not show a cardiovascular benefit. So even within "prescription omega-3," not all formulations behave the same way. EPA-only at the trial dose was the regimen that worked; EPA + DHA mixtures were not.

OTC fish oil supplements are mostly EPA + DHA mixtures, at lower and more variable doses, with regulatory oversight as supplements rather than as drugs. They have not demonstrated REDUCE-IT-level cardiovascular benefit.

**Why you might be a REDUCE-IT-style candidate.** The FDA-approved indication for icosapent ethyl is for adults on statin therapy with triglycerides >=150 mg/dL who either have established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease or have diabetes plus two additional cardiovascular risk factors. Your profile — prior MI, type 2 diabetes, on atorvastatin, TG around 220 — fits this indication directly. That's likely what your cardiologist was thinking.

**Side-effect signals to know about.** REDUCE-IT showed a modest increase in new-onset atrial fibrillation and a modest increase in bleeding events on icosapent ethyl. Neither was huge in absolute terms, but both are worth flagging:
- The atrial-fibrillation signal matters somewhat more for patients with prior structural heart disease.
- The bleeding signal matters somewhat more if you are also on antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel) or anticoagulation.
Most patients in REDUCE-IT tolerated the drug well; nausea, eructation ("fish-oil burps"), and arthralgia are the most common nuisance side effects.

**This does not replace your statin.** Icosapent ethyl is layered on top of statin therapy — not instead of it. You should plan to stay on the atorvastatin.

**Dosing reality.** The trial dose is 4 grams per day, typically 2 g twice daily. That's materially more than what's in most OTC fish-oil pills. Pill count is real; adherence matters.

**Concurrent levers.** Triglycerides also respond to glycemic control, weight management if relevant, physical activity, alcohol moderation, and a Mediterranean-style dietary pattern. None of those *substitute* for icosapent ethyl in your risk profile, but they help.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest discussing initiation with your cardiologist. Your profile fits the REDUCE-IT population, and the evidence is real. If you start, plan to stay on your statin, watch for new palpitations or bleeding, and don't substitute OTC fish oil for the prescription — the trial that succeeded used a specific formulation at a specific dose.
