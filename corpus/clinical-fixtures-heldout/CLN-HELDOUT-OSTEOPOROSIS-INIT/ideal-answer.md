# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-OSTEOPOROSIS-INIT

Good questions and reasonable concerns — let me lay out the picture, including the things you've been reading about.

**Where you are.** A femoral neck T-score of -2.6 and a FRAX 10-year major osteoporotic fracture risk of 22% put you in the "treatment threshold" zone by every major guideline. The Bone Health and Osteoporosis Foundation and Endocrine Society both recommend pharmacologic therapy when T-score is <=-2.5, OR when FRAX 10-year major osteoporotic fracture risk is >=20%, OR when 10-year hip fracture risk is >=3%. You meet both criteria. The expected fracture-risk reduction with treatment is substantial — roughly 40-50% for vertebral fractures, 20-40% for hip fractures depending on the agent.

**Bisphosphonates as first-line.** The standard first-line options are:
- **Oral alendronate** (Fosamax) 70 mg once weekly
- **Oral risedronate** (Actonel) 35 mg weekly or 150 mg monthly
- **IV zoledronic acid** (Reclast) 5 mg once yearly

All three have strong fracture-reduction evidence (zoledronic acid in HORIZON-PFT, alendronate in FIT and others). The choice often comes down to:
- **Tolerability of oral medications** (some patients have GI symptoms or can't reliably stay upright after dosing) — IV zoledronic acid sidesteps both issues.
- **Adherence** — once-yearly IV is much harder to forget than once-weekly oral.
- **Cost / coverage** — alendronate is cheap and generic; zoledronic acid sometimes requires prior auth.

**Typical duration.** This is the part most patients don't know about up front:
- **Oral bisphosphonates**: typically about **5 years**, then a "drug holiday" discussion at that point for lower-risk patients (continued therapy for 7-10 years if you remain at high risk — high T-score deficit, prior fracture, ongoing risk factors).
- **IV zoledronic acid**: typically about **3 years**, then a similar drug-holiday discussion.

The drug holiday concept exists because (a) bisphosphonate effect persists in bone after discontinuation and (b) prolonged exposure is part of what raises atypical femoral fracture risk. Periodic re-assessment with DXA and FRAX guides re-treatment.

**Calcium and vitamin D — the foundation.** Bisphosphonates only work well if calcium and vitamin D are adequate. Aim for **1000-1200 mg of calcium daily** (preferably from diet — dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens — with supplementation only as needed to fill the gap), and **800-1000 IU of vitamin D daily** or whatever it takes to keep your 25-hydroxyvitamin D in the sufficient range. Hypocalcemia at the time of dosing is the main short-term safety concern; your team will likely check calcium and vitamin D before starting, especially before IV zoledronic acid.

**Now — the side effects you're worried about.**

**ONJ (osteonecrosis of the jaw).** Real, but **rare** in osteoporosis treatment. The published estimates in osteoporosis patients are roughly 1 per 10,000 to 1 per 100,000 patient-years — very different from cancer patients on high-dose IV bisphosphonates, where the risk is much higher. Risk modifiers:
- **Invasive dental procedures** (extractions, dental implants, jaw surgery) are the main trigger.
- **Routine dental care should NOT be postponed** because of bisphosphonate use — daily oral hygiene, cleanings, exams reduce risk by preventing infections that otherwise trigger ONJ.
- **If urgent dental work is anticipated, completing it before starting the bisphosphonate is reasonable.**
- Tell any dentist you're on a bisphosphonate when they're planning invasive work.

**Atypical femoral fracture.** Also rare — published estimates in the single digits per 10,000 person-years on long-term therapy. Risk rises with treatment duration, which is part of the reason for drug holidays. **Warning sign**: new thigh or groin pain, sometimes bilateral, that develops without trauma. Tell your clinician immediately if this happens — imaging (often MRI) can identify a stress reaction before it completes into a fracture.

**On both rare risks together.** Across many years and many millions of patient-years of treatment, the **fracture-prevention benefit substantially outweighs** the ONJ and atypical-femoral-fracture risk for patients who meet treatment thresholds. Hip fractures in older women carry one-year mortality around 20%; vertebral fractures carry meaningful chronic morbidity. The math is favorable here. The headlines focus on rare harms because they're frightening, not because they're frequent.

**How to take oral bisphosphonates.** Important — esophagitis is a real risk if you don't follow this:
- **Morning, on an empty stomach.**
- **With a full 8-oz glass of plain water** (not coffee, not juice).
- **Stay upright (sitting or standing) for 30-60 minutes.**
- **No food, other drinks, or other medications** during that interval.
- Then have breakfast and your other medications.

If you have significant GERD, esophageal motility issues, or can't reliably stay upright, IV zoledronic acid sidesteps these issues entirely.

**Denosumab — alternative with a caveat.** Denosumab (Prolia) 60 mg subcutaneous every 6 months is another effective option. Strong fracture-reduction data (FREEDOM trial). The critical caveat: **stopping denosumab is associated with rapid bone loss and rebound vertebral fractures within 6-12 months of the missed dose.** So denosumab is not a "do it for a few years and stop" agent — when discontinuing, transition to a bisphosphonate (often oral alendronate for 1-2 years, or a single dose of IV zoledronic acid) is needed. This is critical and sometimes mishandled. If denosumab is chosen, plan the exit strategy from the start.

**Anabolic agents.** Teriparatide, romosozumab, and abaloparatide are reserved for **very-high-risk** patients — very low T-scores (often <-3 or worse), patients with prior fragility fractures, or those who have failed other therapies. They are not first-line for moderate-to-high-risk patients like you.

**Lifestyle and fall prevention** — concurrent levers:
- **Weight-bearing exercise** and resistance training — bone responds to load.
- **Balance training** (tai chi, specific physical-therapy programs) reduces fall risk.
- **Home fall-proofing** — remove loose rugs, ensure good lighting, install grab bars in bathroom if applicable.
- **Vision check** — uncorrected vision is a major fall-risk factor.
- **Medication review** — sedatives, benzodiazepines, certain antihypertensives, anticholinergics raise fall risk.
- **Smoking cessation** if relevant — directly worsens bone health.
- **Alcohol moderation.**

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest discussing with your clinician which bisphosphonate fits you best — oral alendronate weekly is the most common starting point, with IV zoledronic acid as an alternative if oral dosing is hard to manage. Make sure your calcium / vitamin D / dental status are addressed before starting. Plan for a 5-year (oral) or 3-year (IV) review with a drug-holiday discussion, and use that interval to also be working on exercise, balance, and home safety. The rare side effects you've read about are real but uncommon, and the fracture-prevention math at your risk level clearly favors treatment.
