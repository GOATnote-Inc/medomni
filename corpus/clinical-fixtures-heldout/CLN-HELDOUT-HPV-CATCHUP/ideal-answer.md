The short version: yes, you can still get Gardasil-9 at 35, and for many adults it's a reasonable choice — but the honest answer about how much benefit you'll get is more nuanced than the headlines suggest, so let's walk through it.

**What changed in the recommendations.** Until 2018, HPV vaccination was generally indicated through age 26. In 2018 the FDA expanded the Gardasil-9 indication to ages 27 through 45 for both women and men. Then in 2019 the ACIP (Meites et al., MMWR 2019) updated its recommendation: routine catch-up vaccination is recommended through age 26 for adults not adequately vaccinated, and adults aged 27 through 45 may be vaccinated based on **shared clinical decision-making** with their clinician. That language — "shared decision-making" rather than "routinely recommended" — is deliberate. It reflects that the population-level benefit at older ages is real but smaller, and the right answer depends on the individual.

**What the vaccine actually does — and doesn't do.** Gardasil-9 protects against nine HPV types: 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58. Types 16 and 18 alone are responsible for the majority of cervical cancers worldwide, and the additional five high-risk types in the 9-valent formulation cover most of the rest. Types 6 and 11 cover most genital warts.

But two things the vaccine does *not* do:

1. **It is not therapeutic.** It does not clear an HPV infection you already have, and it does not treat an existing cervical lesion. Its protection is limited to vaccine-covered types you have not yet been exposed to.
2. **It does not replace cervical cancer screening.** Pap testing (with or without primary HPV co-testing) per ACS / USPSTF guidance still applies regardless of vaccination status. That doesn't change.

**Why the benefit at 35 is smaller than at 12.** The pivotal FUTURE I and II trials (Garland and the FUTURE II Study Group, NEJM 2007) demonstrated very high efficacy — around 98% — against vaccine-type-specific high-grade cervical lesions (CIN2+) in participants who were *naive* to those HPV types at the time of vaccination. In already-exposed populations, the efficacy was much lower because the vaccine can't undo prior exposure. By the mid-30s, most sexually active adults have been exposed to at least one HPV type. The catch is that "exposure to some types" doesn't mean exposure to all nine — the protection you'd still get is for whichever covered types you haven't yet acquired. That's why the benefit at 35 is meaningfully diminished compared with vaccination at 12, but it's also why it is not zero.

**Practical details.** For adults 15 and older, Gardasil-9 is a 3-dose series given at 0, 1-2 months, and 6 months. You do **not** need an HPV test or a Pap before vaccination — those aren't prerequisites and shouldn't delay the conversation. Side-effect profile is similar to other adult vaccines: sore arm, occasional low-grade fever, rare allergic reactions. Cost and insurance coverage for the 27-45 indication vary; that's worth checking.

**How to frame the decision with your clinician.** A few questions that genuinely change the math: How many lifetime sexual partners, roughly? Any history of abnormal Pap or known HPV diagnosis? What's your screening situation — when was your last Pap, was it normal, are you up to date? The answers don't determine eligibility, but they help frame how much incremental protection vaccination is likely to add.

**The honest bottom line.** Catch-up vaccination at 35 is a defensible, ACIP-supported, FDA-indicated option. It is not a guarantee that you won't develop cervical cancer — that promise can't be made for any vaccine, especially in someone with likely prior exposure to some HPV types — but it can plausibly reduce your future risk of acquiring a high-risk HPV type you haven't already encountered. Combined with continued routine screening, that's a reasonable risk-reduction strategy. The conversation to have with your clinician is whether the cost, the three injections, and the marginal expected benefit fit your priorities — not whether you're "too old" or "must" get it.
