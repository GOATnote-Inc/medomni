# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-SMOKING-CESSATION-CANCER

This is the gold-standard answer the model should approximate. Every clinical claim traces to a citation in `case.json::expected_sources_referenced`.

---

First, before the medication question — quitting now is one of the most consequential things you can do for your cancer-related prognosis. Continued smoking after a head-and-neck cancer measurably raises the risk of both recurrence and a second primary cancer in the upper airway. So this isn't a routine cessation conversation; it's a cessation conversation where the stakes are unusually high and the upside of getting it right is unusually large. You're already doing the hardest part — wanting to stop — so let's make the medication choice useful.

**Which works better, varenicline or the patch?** The cleanest head-to-head data come from the EAGLES trial (Anthenelli, *Lancet* 2016) — over 8,000 smokers, randomized to varenicline, bupropion, the nicotine patch, or placebo, with both psychiatric and non-psychiatric cohorts. EAGLES found that **varenicline was the most effective single agent**, with continuous abstinence (weeks 9-24) higher than the patch, bupropion, or placebo. Bupropion and the patch were roughly comparable to each other, and both were clearly better than placebo. So if the choice is varenicline alone vs the patch alone, varenicline tends to do better.

**But there's a wrinkle that changes the answer.** The 2008 Public Health Service Clinical Practice Guideline (Fiore et al., the standard reference) and subsequent meta-analyses found that **combination NRT** — the patch plus a short-acting form (lozenge, gum, or inhaler) — is roughly as effective as varenicline. The patch handles the steady baseline craving; the lozenge or gum handles breakthrough urges. Used together, they close most of the efficacy gap with varenicline. So "varenicline vs the patch alone" undersells what NRT can do.

**Is one safer than the other?** This is where there's been real misinformation that's worth correcting directly. For years, varenicline carried a boxed warning about possible serious mental-health effects — depression, suicidal thoughts. EAGLES was specifically designed to test that signal, including a large cohort of smokers with stable psychiatric conditions. The result: **no significant increase in moderate-to-severe neuropsychiatric adverse events with varenicline (or bupropion) compared with the patch or placebo**, in either the psychiatric or non-psychiatric cohort. On the strength of those data, the FDA removed varenicline's boxed warning in 2016. So the older reputation hasn't kept up with the evidence.

What you *will* notice on each:
- **Varenicline**: nausea is the most common — often mild, often better if you take it with food, and usually improves once the dose stabilizes. Vivid dreams and some insomnia are also common.
- **Nicotine patch**: skin irritation at the patch site, occasional vivid dreams, and (if you wear it overnight) some sleep disruption. Removing it before bed is one option.
- **Combination NRT**: same as patch above, plus mouth/throat irritation from short-acting forms.

**A piece that often makes the biggest difference, regardless of which medication you pick.** Behavioral support — counseling, the national quitline (1-800-QUIT-NOW), text-based programs, and structured cessation apps — meaningfully increases the odds of staying quit when paired with medication. The USPSTF (2021, Grade A recommendation) endorses pharmacotherapy *plus* behavioral support for adult smokers; the combination outperforms either alone. Medication isn't the whole plan; counseling isn't the whole plan; the pair is.

**Practical framing for the conversation with your clinician.** Reasonable options:
- Varenicline alone — strongest single-agent data; expect some nausea early.
- Combination NRT (patch + lozenge or gum) — roughly comparable efficacy; over-the-counter; convenient.
- Bupropion — useful especially if there's also low mood or weight-gain concern; avoid if seizure risk.

Cost, insurance coverage, prior quit attempts, and how each option felt last time (if there was a last time) all matter. There's no "wrong" answer among these — there's a best fit for you.

**One last thing.** Quitting is often a multi-attempt process; that's not failure, that's the norm. Each attempt teaches something useful. The fact that you've reached out and are asking which tool to pick is exactly the right step.
