# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-DOAC-AFIB

For someone in your situation — atrial fibrillation, age 71, hypertension, diabetes, prior TIA — anticoagulation isn't a "should I or shouldn't I" question. It's a definite yes. The remaining question is which one, and for non-valvular AFib (which is what you have) current guidelines favor a DOAC over warfarin.

**Why anticoagulation at all.** Atrial fibrillation lets blood pool in the left atrial appendage, where clots can form and embolize, most dangerously to the brain. Your CHA2DS2-VASc score — a tool that estimates yearly stroke risk — adds points for age >=65, hypertension, diabetes, and prior stroke/TIA. At a score of 4 (one of those scoring 2 because of your TIA), your projected annual stroke risk without anticoagulation is roughly 4-5%. Anticoagulation reduces that risk substantially.

**DOAC vs warfarin for non-valvular AFib.** The 2024 AHA/ACC/HRS atrial fibrillation guideline recommends a DOAC over warfarin for non-valvular AFib in patients who don't have a mechanical heart valve or moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis. The four DOACs each had pivotal trials versus warfarin:

- **ARISTOTLE** (Granger et al., NEJM 2011) — apixaban: superior to warfarin for stroke/systemic embolism, lower major bleeding, lower mortality.
- **RE-LY** (Connolly et al., NEJM 2009) — dabigatran 150 mg BID: superior stroke prevention vs warfarin; similar major bleeding.
- **ROCKET-AF** (Patel et al., NEJM 2011) — rivaroxaban: non-inferior to warfarin; similar bleeding profile.
- **ENGAGE-AF** (Giugliano et al., NEJM 2013) — edoxaban: non-inferior to warfarin for stroke prevention; lower major bleeding.

Across all four, intracranial hemorrhage was substantially reduced compared with warfarin — that's the most catastrophic anticoagulation-related event, and it's where DOACs really earn their preference.

**Apixaban specifics.** Standard dose is 5 mg twice daily. The dose is reduced to 2.5 mg twice daily if the patient meets at least two of these three criteria: age >=80, body weight <=60 kg, serum creatinine >=1.5 mg/dL. So your dose will depend on your weight and kidney function, which your team will check.

**The exception worth knowing.** If you had a mechanical heart valve or moderate-to-severe mitral stenosis (rheumatic), warfarin would still be preferred — DOACs aren't appropriate in those settings. Your AFib sounds non-valvular, so this exception doesn't apply.

**Practical differences.**

- **Warfarin** requires INR monitoring with a target of 2-3 and has many drug and food interactions (vitamin K, antibiotics, amiodarone, many others). It's been the workhorse for decades. It's cheap.
- **DOACs** don't require routine INR checks but DO require periodic kidney function and complete blood count. They have fewer drug interactions than warfarin but not zero — strong CYP3A4 / P-glycoprotein inhibitors and inducers matter, and combinations with antiplatelet drugs raise bleeding risk.
- **HAS-BLED** is a bleeding-risk score; it's used to flag and modify risk factors (uncontrolled hypertension, NSAID use, alcohol), not to withhold anticoagulation that you otherwise need.

**Reversal.** Warfarin: vitamin K, prothrombin complex concentrate. Dabigatran: idarucizumab. Apixaban / rivaroxaban: andexanet alfa (where available; cost and availability vary). All major US centers have a reversal protocol.

**The bleeding piece is real.** Any anticoagulant raises bleeding risk — GI bleeding is the most common, intracranial hemorrhage is the most feared. Modifiable factors worth addressing: tight blood-pressure control, avoid concurrent NSAIDs, fall-risk-reduction at home, alcohol moderation. If you're taking aspirin for any reason and don't have a separate cardiovascular indication, that should be reviewed.

**Bottom line.** For your specific profile, I'd suggest discussing apixaban with your cardiologist as the leading option. The trial data, the lower intracranial-hemorrhage signal, and the convenience of fixed dosing make it a reasonable default. Warfarin is still a perfectly defensible choice — especially if cost, monitoring access, or other clinical factors favor it — but for non-valvular AFib in 2026, DOAC-first is the recommended path.
