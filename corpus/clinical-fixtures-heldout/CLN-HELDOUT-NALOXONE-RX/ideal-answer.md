# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-NALOXONE-RX

Good question, and an understandable reaction. The naloxone prescription isn't a comment on you — it's standard practice now for anyone on a meaningful daily opioid dose, exactly the way smoke detectors are standard for anyone who has a kitchen. You aren't being accused of anything. You're being given a tool that can save your life or someone else's.

**Why your clinician offered it.** Under the CDC 2022 opioid prescribing guideline, naloxone co-prescription is recommended for patients on >=50 morphine-milligram-equivalents per day, those on opioids alongside benzodiazepines, those with a personal or family history of overdose or substance-use disorder, and those at otherwise elevated overdose risk (sleep apnea, advanced age, certain pulmonary disease). At ~60 MME/day, you're above the threshold. This is reflexive, evidence-based practice — not a flag on you specifically.

**One important update.** As of March 2023, intranasal naloxone (Narcan, 4 mg nasal spray) is FDA-approved for over-the-counter sale. So you don't need a prescription to get more — you can pick up additional doses at most pharmacies directly off the shelf. Many clinics also distribute it free.

**Recognizing an opioid overdose.** Naloxone is for someone who can't speak for themselves, so this matters most for the people around you. Signs to watch for:
- **Unresponsive** — won't wake up, won't respond to a sternal rub or shoulder shake.
- **Very slow or absent breathing** — fewer than ~10 breaths per minute, gasping, or no breathing.
- **Blue or gray lips, fingertips, or face.**
- **Pinpoint pupils** — very small, dilated only reluctantly.
- Limp body, gurgling or choking sounds.

If your partner, an adult child, or someone who's around you knows these signs, they're who will use the naloxone, not you.

**How to use intranasal Narcan.**

1. **Peel open the package.** It's a single-use sprayer pre-loaded with one dose.
2. **Position the person on their back.** Hold the device with thumb on the plunger and first/middle fingers along the nozzle.
3. **Insert the nozzle into either nostril** until your fingers touch the bottom of their nose.
4. **Press the plunger firmly all the way in** to release the dose. The whole dose goes in one push.
5. **Move the person to the recovery position** — on their side, top knee bent forward, head tilted slightly back to keep the airway open.
6. **Call 911 immediately** if it hasn't been called already.
7. **Wait 2-3 minutes.** If they don't start breathing or wake up, give a second dose in the other nostril. Have a second device ready.
8. **Stay with them.** Even after they wake up.

**The 911 step is non-negotiable.** Naloxone wears off in 30-90 minutes. Many opioids — especially long-acting formulations and fentanyl — last longer than that. Once naloxone's effect fades, the person can slip back into respiratory depression. Emergency services and a hospital observation period are part of the plan, not optional.

**Good Samaritan laws.** Most US states have laws that provide some legal protection for bystanders who call for help during an overdose — protection from prosecution for things like drug possession when someone is calling 911 in good faith. The exact protections vary by state, but the point is: nobody calling for help on your behalf should hesitate over legal worry. Tell your household this.

**Where to keep it.**
- A known, easily accessible spot in the home — bedside table, kitchen, anywhere a household member can reach in under a minute.
- Tell whoever lives with you (and ideally a frequently-visiting friend or family member) that it's there and quickly walk them through how it works.
- Check the expiration date. If it expires, replace it — Narcan is OTC now, so this is easy.
- Consider keeping a second device in a bag or vehicle if you spend a lot of time outside the home.

**What can go wrong with using it.** Almost nothing. Naloxone is essentially harmless if given to someone who isn't actually having an opioid overdose. The main downside in someone *with* opioid dependence is that it precipitates withdrawal — uncomfortable, not dangerous. The right move is always to administer if there's reasonable suspicion of overdose; you don't need to be sure.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest taking the prescription as a routine safety measure, picking up a second device OTC so you have backup, and walking your household through the steps tonight. The prescription doesn't change anything about how you take your hydromorphone or how your team treats you — it just means there's a working safety net in the home.
