Two real questions, and they deserve to be answered in order — first the cancer worry, because that's what's in the way, and then the actual statin decision.

**Is the cancer risk real?** That concern circulates online, and it's a fair thing to ask before starting any long-term medication. The honest answer based on the evidence is no — large pooled analyses of statin trials have not found an increase in cancer risk. The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' (CTT) Collaboration combined data from the major statin randomized trials — tens of thousands of patient-years — and reported in *Lancet* (2010 and 2012) that statin therapy was *not* associated with increased cancer incidence and was *not* associated with increased cancer mortality. That's about as strong a body of evidence as we have for this kind of question. So the cancer concern, while understandable, isn't supported by the data.

That doesn't mean statins have *no* side effects. The real, established harms are different from cancer:

- **Muscle aches** in some people (mostly mild; severe rhabdomyolysis is rare).
- **Mild, reversible elevations in liver tests**, usually without symptoms.
- **A small absolute increase in new-onset type 2 diabetes**, on the order of 1 extra case per ~250 person-years on therapy.

Those are the harms worth discussing — not cancer.

**Should you start a statin?** That's a decision for you and your physician, but here's the framework most guidelines use.

Your 10-year cardiovascular risk is 12%. That number puts you squarely in the range where:

- The **US Preventive Services Task Force (2022)** recommends statin therapy for primary prevention in adults aged 40-75 with at least one cardiovascular risk factor and a 10-year risk of 10% or higher (Grade B recommendation).
- The **2018 AHA/ACC/Multisociety cholesterol guideline** places you in an intermediate-to-high-risk band, where the next step is reviewing risk-enhancing factors (family history of premature cardiovascular disease, persistently elevated LDL — that's "bad cholesterol," chronic kidney disease, metabolic syndrome, inflammatory conditions, ancestry-specific factors) and discussing a statin.

The trial that most directly speaks to primary prevention in someone like you is JUPITER (Ridker, NEJM 2008): rosuvastatin versus placebo in adults without prior cardiovascular disease but with an elevated marker of inflammation. It showed a meaningful relative reduction in major cardiovascular events, on the order of 40-50%.

**What that means in absolute terms — which is what matters to you.** A 12% 10-year risk, reduced by roughly a quarter to a third with a statin, becomes something like an 8-9% risk. That's roughly a **3-4 percentage-point absolute reduction over 10 years** — for many people that's worth taking a daily pill for; for others it isn't. Both answers are reasonable. What matters is the trade-off being yours, not someone else's.

**A statin is one tool, not the whole plan.** Diet, regular activity, blood pressure, and — if relevant — smoking cessation typically move risk meaningfully on their own, and they compound with statin therapy. It's also worth confirming with your physician whether any risk-enhancers apply to you, because they can shift the calculation either way.

**Two pieces of honest uncertainty.** No individual is guaranteed benefit — statins reduce risk on average across populations, but any specific person may or may not have been the one whose event was prevented. And recommendations can shift as new data arrive, which is part of why "discuss with your clinician" isn't a cop-out — it's the right way to land this.

So: the cancer concern isn't supported by the evidence. The cardiovascular case for at least a serious conversation about a statin, given your 12% number, is solid. The remaining question is whether the absolute benefit, the realistic side-effect profile, and your own preferences add up to "yes" for you — and that's the conversation worth having with your physician.
