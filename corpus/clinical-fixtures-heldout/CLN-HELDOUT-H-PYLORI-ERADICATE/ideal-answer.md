# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-H-PYLORI-ERADICATE

Good question, and timely to ask before you start — this is one of those areas where the recommendations have meaningfully shifted in the last few years. Here's the current picture.

**Why the regimen has changed.** Until recently, the default H. pylori regimen in the US was "triple therapy" — a PPI plus amoxicillin plus clarithromycin for 10-14 days. The trouble is, clarithromycin resistance has crossed the 15% threshold in most US regions, and the ACG 2024 guideline (Chey et al., Am J Gastroenterol 2024) now recommends against using clarithromycin-based triple therapy as universal first-line because eradication rates have fallen well below 80% in too many places. So the field has moved to bismuth-based quadruple therapy, with several alternatives.

**The current first-line in most of the US: bismuth quadruple therapy for 14 days.** Components:
- A **PPI** (e.g., omeprazole 40 mg twice daily, or equivalent) — twice daily.
- **Bismuth subsalicylate or subcitrate** — typically 524 mg four times daily (Pepto-Bismol-style for the subsalicylate).
- **Tetracycline** 500 mg four times daily.
- **Metronidazole** 250-500 mg three or four times daily.

That's a lot of pills four times a day for two weeks. Annoying, but it works.

**Alternative first-line options.**
- **Vonoprazan-based regimens** (Voquezna). Vonoprazan is a potassium-competitive acid blocker that achieves more profound and faster acid suppression than PPIs. FDA-approved in 2022 for H. pylori eradication, available as vonoprazan + amoxicillin (dual) or vonoprazan + amoxicillin + clarithromycin (triple), 14 days.
- **Rifabutin triple therapy** (Talicia — rifabutin + amoxicillin + omeprazole, all in fixed-dose capsules four times daily for 14 days). Useful where macrolide resistance and susceptibility data are uncertain.
- **Levofloxacin-based regimens** are typically reserved for salvage after one prior failure, not first-line.

The choice often depends on insurance, pill-burden tolerance, and your particular GI clinic's preferences. Bismuth quadruple is the most common because it's well-studied, generic, and covered by virtually all insurance.

**The 14-day duration matters.** Meta-analyses have consistently shown 14-day courses outperform shorter (7-day, 10-day) courses for H. pylori eradication. **Don't stop early because you feel better.** Stomach symptoms often improve within the first few days; the bacteria are not yet eradicated. Incomplete adherence is one of the top causes of treatment failure and contributes to resistance.

**Side effects to expect with bismuth quadruple.**
- **Black tongue and black stools** from the bismuth — harmless, freaks people out, mention it so you know it's expected.
- **Metallic taste** from metronidazole.
- **No alcohol** during and for several days after metronidazole — disulfiram-like reaction (severe nausea/vomiting/flushing) is real and unpleasant.
- Mild nausea, diarrhea, abdominal cramping — common, usually tolerable.
- Tetracycline causes photosensitivity — wear sunscreen, avoid prolonged sun exposure during the course.

**The test of cure — and timing matters a lot.** After completing the 14 days, you need to confirm that the H. pylori was actually eradicated. Two key requirements:

1. **Wait at least 4 weeks** after completing the antibiotics before testing. Earlier testing can show false negatives because the residual bacterial load may be temporarily suppressed.
2. **Be off PPI therapy for at least 2 weeks** (and off bismuth and antibiotics) before testing. PPI suppresses bacterial activity and causes false negatives on urea breath and stool antigen testing.

The right tests:
- **Urea breath test** — non-invasive, reliable for confirming eradication.
- **Stool antigen test** — non-invasive, also reliable.
- **NOT serology** — antibodies stay positive for years after successful eradication, so a positive serology after treatment is uninformative.
- Repeat endoscopy with biopsy is sometimes done if a follow-up endoscopy is indicated for ulcer healing or alarm features anyway.

**Ulcer-healing follow-up.** Duodenal ulcers typically heal with H. pylori eradication plus continued acid suppression. Many GI clinics keep patients on a once-daily PPI for a total of 4-8 weeks for duodenal ulcers (sometimes longer for gastric ulcers, which also typically get a follow-up endoscopy to confirm healing).

**If the test of cure is positive.** That happens in 10-20% of patients on first-line therapy and is not a personal failure — it usually reflects resistance. The next step is a **salvage regimen** that does *not* repeat the antibiotics from the failed course (so if you got bismuth quadruple, salvage is often a vonoprazan-based or rifabutin-based regimen). Your GI clinic will guide that.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest confirming with the GI office whether they're putting you on bismuth quadruple, vonoprazan-based, or rifabutin-based therapy, then completing the full 14 days strictly, then scheduling a urea breath test or stool antigen test approximately 6 weeks later (4 weeks after the antibiotics + 2 weeks off the PPI). If the test confirms eradication, the duodenal ulcer should heal on continued acid suppression.
