# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-PCSK9-PRIOR-AUTH

Yes, this is exactly the situation PCSK9 inhibitors were designed for, and yes — insurance will probably ask for paperwork before they say yes. Here's the picture.

**What PCSK9 inhibitors do.** Your LDL receptors are how your liver pulls LDL out of your bloodstream. PCSK9 is a protein that tags those receptors for destruction. PCSK9 inhibitors block that tagging — so the receptors live longer, the liver clears more LDL, and your circulating LDL drops. On top of a statin (which also raises LDL receptor activity), a PCSK9 inhibitor typically lowers LDL another 50-60%. From your current 138, that would likely put you in the 55-70 range.

**The cardiovascular-outcomes evidence.** Two big trials anchor the cardiovascular case. FOURIER (Sabatine et al., NEJM 2017) randomized about 27,000 statin-treated patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease to evolocumab or placebo and showed a roughly 15% relative reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events. ODYSSEY OUTCOMES (Schwartz et al., NEJM 2018) did similarly with alirocumab in a post-ACS population. Neither trial demonstrated an all-cause-mortality benefit, but both showed reductions in non-fatal cardiovascular events — meaningful, not transformational.

**Two classes worth knowing about.** Alirocumab and evolocumab are monoclonal antibodies, given as subcutaneous injections every 2 to 4 weeks (depending on the dose). Inclisiran is a different mechanism — a small interfering RNA that suppresses PCSK9 production in the liver — given as an injection at baseline, 3 months, and then every 6 months. Inclisiran has shorter outcomes follow-up than the monoclonals; ORION-10 and ORION-11 demonstrated LDL reduction comparable to the monoclonals.

**Why HeFH matters here.** Heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia is one of the populations specifically called out by the 2018 AHA/ACC cholesterol guideline as a candidate for non-statin intensification when LDL stays elevated despite maximally tolerated statin (with or without ezetimibe). LDL 138 on rosuvastatin 40 + ezetimibe in someone with HeFH is exactly the documented scenario most plans recognize for PCSK9 prior authorization.

**The prior-auth reality.** Honest answer: insurance typically asks for documentation of (a) your HeFH diagnosis (clinical criteria like Dutch Lipid Clinic Network or Simon Broome, or genetic confirmation), (b) that you've actually been on maximally tolerated statin, (c) often that you've also been on ezetimibe, and (d) that your LDL is still above the plan's threshold. Some plans also want a recent lipid panel within a defined window. Approval is common in this exact scenario but not automatic, and appeals do sometimes happen. Your clinic's lipid pharmacist or PA team will likely run point on this.

**Tolerability.** Generally well-tolerated. The most common adverse effect is an injection-site reaction (mild redness or soreness), occasional flu-like symptoms. Cognitive concerns from earlier observational data did not bear out in the prospective trials.

**Don't drop the statin or the ezetimibe.** PCSK9 inhibitors are *additive* — they layer on top of the regimen you're already on, not in place of it. That's exactly how the evidence base was built.

**Adjacent items worth raising.** Bempedoic acid (oral; CLEAR Outcomes 2023 demonstrated CV-event reduction) is another non-statin lever, sometimes used in addition to or instead of ezetimibe. Cascade screening of your first-degree relatives is also part of HeFH care — siblings, children, parents who haven't been screened deserve a lipid panel.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest discussing initiation with your lipid clinic — this is the population the drugs are designed for and the prior-auth case is strong, but plan on the paperwork taking a few weeks.
