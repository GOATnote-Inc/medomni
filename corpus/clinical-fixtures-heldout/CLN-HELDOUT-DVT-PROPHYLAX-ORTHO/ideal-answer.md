# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-DVT-PROPHYLAX-ORTHO

Both questions are reasonable, and the answer depends on a couple of things you should bring back to your orthopedic team rather than decide unilaterally. The short version: keep going past day 8 — most guidelines recommend at least 10-14 days, and many patients are extended out to about 35 days. And yes, aspirin is a legitimate option in lower-risk patients now, but it's not interchangeable for everyone.

**How long do you actually need prophylaxis after a knee replacement?** The risk of postoperative venous thromboembolism (DVT or PE) is highest in the first 1-2 weeks after major orthopedic surgery and remains elevated for several weeks. The major guideline frameworks — ASH 2019, CHEST 2022, AAOS 2024 — recommend a minimum of 10-14 days of pharmacologic prophylaxis after total knee arthroplasty, with extension up to about 35 days (the longer duration is often used for total hip arthroplasty, but is also offered to TKA patients with additional risk factors like prior VTE, obesity, malignancy, or limited mobility). Eight days isn't long enough; don't stop yet.

**Aspirin — what changed?** A few years ago, aspirin would have been considered marginal for orthopedic VTE prophylaxis. Two newer trials and the updated AAOS 2024 CPG shifted that:

- **EPCAT-II** (Anderson et al., NEJM 2018) randomized roughly 3,400 patients undergoing total hip or total knee arthroplasty who had received 5 days of postoperative rivaroxaban to either continued rivaroxaban or aspirin 81 mg daily for the remainder of the prophylaxis window. Aspirin was non-inferior for symptomatic VTE with a similar bleeding profile. This was the trial that made aspirin a defensible option in lower-risk patients.
- **CRISTAL** (Sidhu et al., JAMA 2022) compared aspirin alone to enoxaparin for VTE prophylaxis in primary hip or knee arthroplasty patients in Australia. Aspirin was *not* non-inferior — symptomatic VTE was higher with aspirin alone. This pulled the pendulum back toward "aspirin is reasonable in lower-risk patients, not in everyone."

The AAOS 2024 CPG endorses aspirin as one of several acceptable agents for VTE prophylaxis after TKA in standard-risk patients, alongside DOACs, LMWH, fondaparinux, and warfarin. The choice is risk-stratified.

**What that means for you specifically.** It is reasonable to ask your orthopedic team whether the EPCAT-II-style approach — finish a defined initial DOAC course, then transition to aspirin for the remainder — fits your risk profile. Important inputs they will weigh:

- Personal or family history of DVT/PE
- Body mass index, mobility, presence of malignancy
- Bleeding risk factors (history of GI bleeding, ulcer disease, age)
- Whether you tolerate the rivaroxaban and have any wound issues
- Their center's protocol — many centers have moved toward DOAC-then-aspirin pathways post-TJA; others stick with full-course DOAC or LMWH

**Don't switch on your own.** This is a conversation with the surgeon's office, not a self-directed change.

**Mechanical adjuncts matter.** Pharmacologic prophylaxis sits on top of a mechanical foundation: early ambulation as physical therapy permits, ankle-pump exercises, intermittent pneumatic compression devices in early days, calf and quadriceps activation. Don't sit immobile for hours.

**Symptoms to call about right away.**

- **DVT:** unilateral calf swelling, warmth, tenderness, or redness (especially asymmetric — one calf bigger or warmer than the other).
- **PE:** sudden shortness of breath, pleuritic (sharp, breathing-related) chest pain, fast heart rate, lightheadedness, coughing up blood. **PE symptoms are a 911 / ER call, not an outpatient call.**

**Bleeding to flag.** Wound drainage that doesn't ease with time, easy bruising larger than expected, blood in stool or urine, dark tarry stools, severe nosebleed. Tell the surgical team.

**Other interactions to know.** While you're on rivaroxaban: avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) unless specifically cleared, limit alcohol, and double-check any new medications your other clinicians prescribe — strong CYP3A4 / P-glycoprotein interactions affect rivaroxaban levels.

**Honest uncertainty.** Practice on this is genuinely evolving, and surgeon-to-surgeon variation in extended-prophylaxis protocols is real. The "right" duration and the "right" agent depend on details about your specific risk profile that I don't have.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest calling the orthopedic team today to confirm: (1) total planned duration of prophylaxis (typically 14-35 days post-TKA depending on your risk profile), and (2) whether their pathway supports a switch from rivaroxaban to aspirin after a defined initial period in your specific case. Don't stop and don't switch on your own.
