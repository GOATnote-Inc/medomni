The short answer: aspirin is not the right first step here, and the recommendation has changed in ways that haven't fully filtered into the public conversation yet. Two things deserve to come first — a genetic evaluation, and a colonoscopy schedule built around your family history.

**Why the recommendation has shifted.** In 2016 the US Preventive Services Task Force suggested that low-dose aspirin might prevent both heart attacks and colorectal cancer in some adults in their 50s. In 2022 the Task Force updated that position. With more data — especially from the ASPREE trial (McNeil et al., NEJM 2018) of more than 19,000 healthy older adults — the cancer-prevention claim no longer held up, and bleeding harms looked larger than previously thought. ASPREE actually showed *increased* all-cause mortality and *increased* major bleeding on aspirin, with no cancer-mortality benefit. So the current USPSTF position no longer recommends aspirin primarily to prevent colon cancer in average-risk adults.

**Where aspirin still might matter — and why it doesn't automatically apply to you yet.** The strongest evidence that aspirin reduces colon cancer comes from the CAPP2 trial (Burn et al., Lancet 2011, with 10-year follow-up in Lancet 2020), which studied people with **Lynch syndrome** — a specific inherited cancer-risk condition. In CAPP2, daily aspirin (at 600 mg/day, much higher than a "baby aspirin") cut colon-cancer incidence roughly in half over a decade. That's a striking finding, but it doesn't transfer directly to you for two reasons: (1) we don't yet know whether you carry a hereditary syndrome, and (2) the dose was different.

**What should come first, given your family history.** Two first-degree relatives with colon cancer — both diagnosed under or around 60 — is exactly the pattern that warrants:

1. **Genetic counseling and Lynch-syndrome testing**, ideally starting with tumor testing on an affected relative if available, or a multi-gene hereditary-cancer panel for you. The result genuinely changes what we do next.
2. **Earlier and more frequent colonoscopy.** The US Multi-Society Task Force recommends, for someone with two first-degree relatives with colorectal cancer, starting screening at age 40 — or 10 years before the youngest affected relative's diagnosis, whichever comes first — and repeating roughly every 5 years rather than every 10. For you, that means you're already at the age where a colonoscopy should be on the calendar if it isn't already. **Colonoscopy is the dominant risk-reducer here. Aspirin is, at best, additive — not a substitute.**

**The bleeding piece.** Daily low-dose aspirin raises the risk of major gastrointestinal bleeding by roughly 50-60% in relative terms, and adds a small absolute increase in hemorrhagic stroke. Those harms grow with age, with NSAID use, with anticoagulants, and with prior GI bleeding history. They're real and they shape the math.

**Honest uncertainty.** Even setting all of that aside, translating CAPP2's high-dose, Lynch-specific finding to a 52-year-old with familial-but-not-yet-confirmed-syndromic risk is not a clean fit. That's a real limit of the current evidence.

**So what I'd suggest discussing with your physician**, in this order: (a) genetic counseling and a hereditary-cancer-panel decision, (b) a colonoscopy plan tailored to your family history, (c) cardiovascular and bleeding-risk assessment, and only then (d) whether aspirin has any role for you specifically — a decision that should follow, not lead. The answer for you may end up being yes, no, or "yes if Lynch is confirmed and bleeding risk is low." But it shouldn't be answered before steps (a) and (b).
