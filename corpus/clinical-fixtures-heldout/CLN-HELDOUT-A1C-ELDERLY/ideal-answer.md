# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-A1C-ELDERLY

The instinct that something might be off here is the right one. For your mom — 82, with mild cognitive impairment, having two hypoglycemic episodes in six months — the answer almost certainly is *not* "tighter sugars." If anything, it's the opposite: the regimen is too tight for her risk-benefit profile, and the conversation to have with her clinician is about pulling back, not pushing harder.

**What the guidelines actually say for older adults.** The ADA Standards of Care has a specific section on older adults that recommends individualizing the A1c goal based on health status. For an 82-year-old with cognitive impairment and recurrent hypoglycemia, the recommended A1c range is generally less stringent than for a healthy younger adult — commonly described as <8%, and for those with significant multimorbidity, frailty, or limited life expectancy, <8.5% is also acceptable. Her A1c at 7.4% is already on the lower edge of where she should be living. Tightening below 7% in her profile increases hypoglycemia risk without commensurate benefit. The big trials behind this stance — ACCORD especially — actually showed harm from intensive glycemic control in older / high-risk patients.

**The hypoglycemia is the signal.** Two events in six months — one symptomatic, one only caught by CGM — is exactly the pattern that prompts deintensification. Hypoglycemia in older adults is not a benign event. It causes falls and fractures, cardiovascular events, can accelerate cognitive decline, and nocturnal hypoglycemia in particular is dangerous because it is often unrecognized. The CGM did exactly what CGM is for here.

**The medication conversation.** Glipizide is a sulfonylurea, and sulfonylureas in older adults are one of the highest-risk classes for hypoglycemia. The conversation worth having with her clinician is whether glipizide should be tapered or replaced with an agent that doesn't typically cause hypoglycemia — for example a DPP-4 inhibitor (sitagliptin, linagliptin), a GLP-1 receptor agonist (depending on weight, GI tolerance, and access), or an SGLT2 inhibitor (depending on her renal function and volume status). Metformin is usually fine to continue in older adults, including down to an eGFR of 30 with appropriate caution; it does not by itself cause hypoglycemia.

**Practical things that will help right now.**

- Ensure her home aide knows how to recognize hypoglycemia symptoms and what to do.
- Make sure glucagon is available at home (the nasal Baqsimi or injectable forms) and that the aide and family know how to use it.
- Continue the CGM — for someone with cognitive impairment and an unrecognized nocturnal event, CGM is the right tool and her clinician can use the alarm settings.
- Review medications generally — some other meds (beta-blockers, alcohol, certain antibiotics) can blunt or worsen hypoglycemia signaling.
- Review driving safety. If she still drives at all, recurrent hypoglycemia is a real consideration.

**To restate the framing.** "Should her sugars be tighter?" — almost certainly no. "Is something off?" — yes, in the sense that she is having avoidable hypoglycemia at an A1c that is already appropriately controlled for her profile. The right next move is a medication-simplification conversation with her primary or geriatric clinician, not a drive toward a lower number.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest scheduling a focused diabetes-medication review with her clinician. Bring the CGM data with you. The likely outcome is a tapered glipizide and a switch toward an agent with lower hypoglycemia risk, with the A1c target re-set into the 7.5-8% range that fits her age and clinical context.
