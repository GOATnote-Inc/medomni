# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-PREP-HIV

Yes — your friend is right. You're in exactly the population PrEP was designed for, and the USPSTF gave PrEP a Grade A recommendation in 2023, which is their highest level of recommendation. Let me walk you through what's actually involved.

**Three FDA-approved PrEP regimens.** You have options:

1. **Daily oral TDF/FTC (Truvada / generic).** One pill every day. The original PrEP regimen, supported by iPrEx (Grant et al., NEJM 2010). >99% effective at preventing HIV with consistent use. Cheap (generic).
2. **Daily oral TAF/FTC (Descovy).** One pill every day. Similar efficacy to Truvada with a slightly more favorable renal and bone safety profile (DISCOVER, Mayer et al., Lancet 2020). FDA-approved for MSM and transgender women but **not** for people at risk through receptive vaginal exposure (the trial didn't include them).
3. **Long-acting injectable cabotegravir (Apretude).** Intramuscular gluteal injection given as 2 loading doses a month apart, then every 2 months. HPTN 083 (Landovitz et al., NEJM 2021) found cabotegravir was *superior* to daily oral TDF/FTC for HIV prevention, largely because injection adherence is more reliable than daily pills.

Any of the three is an evidence-supported option for you as an MSM patient.

**A fourth option worth knowing for MSM specifically: event-driven dosing.** If you don't have sex daily but can plan ahead, the "2-1-1" regimen — 2 pills of TDF/FTC 2-24 hours before sex, 1 pill 24 hours after, 1 pill 48 hours after — is an alternative supported by the IPERGAY trial (Molina et al., NEJM 2015). It's specifically validated for MSM. It's not recommended for cisgender or transgender women whose primary exposure is receptive vaginal intercourse, where steady-state pill levels matter more.

**One absolute requirement before starting.** You need a confirmed HIV-negative status at baseline. PrEP regimens are not full HIV treatment regimens — if you start PrEP while actually carrying HIV (especially acute, recently-acquired HIV that may not show on every test), you can develop drug-resistant HIV. Baseline testing typically includes:
- 4th-generation HIV antigen/antibody test (and an HIV RNA test if there's any suspicion of acute infection — fevers, rash, lymphadenopathy in the past few weeks).
- Renal function (creatinine, eGFR) — important especially for TDF/FTC regimens.
- Hepatitis B serology — because TDF/FTC and TAF/FTC have anti-HBV activity, and stopping them in an undiagnosed HBV patient can trigger a flare.
- STI screening — gonorrhea, chlamydia (at urethral, rectal, and pharyngeal sites depending on practices), syphilis, hepatitis C.

**Ongoing follow-up.** Roughly every 3 months: HIV testing, STI screening, adherence check, prescription refill. For TDF/FTC, periodic renal function. For cabotegravir, the visit is also when you get the next injection.

**Side effects.** Most people on oral PrEP tolerate it well. The most common early effects are mild GI upset (nausea, occasional loose stools) and headache, usually resolving within 2-4 weeks. TDF/FTC has small effects on renal function and bone mineral density that mostly matter over years; TAF/FTC has a more favorable profile in those domains but slightly more weight gain and lipid effects. Cabotegravir injections cause injection-site reactions (soreness, sometimes a small lump) in a meaningful fraction of patients, usually mild.

**Doxy-PEP — separate but adjacent.** PrEP prevents HIV. It does not prevent gonorrhea, chlamydia, or syphilis. Doxy-PEP (200 mg of doxycycline taken within 72 hours after condomless sex) is a distinct but adjacent strategy that has been shown to substantially reduce bacterial STI acquisition in MSM and transgender women, and is now part of CDC guidance for selected patients. Worth asking about at the same visit.

**Access.** Under USPSTF Grade A, most US insurance plans cover PrEP without cost-sharing — including labs and visits. The federal **Ready, Set, PrEP** program provides PrEP medication free for uninsured / underinsured patients. Many sexual health clinics offer same-visit initiation if your screening labs are reassuring.

**A note on framing.** Asking about PrEP is a self-care decision, not a moral one. The decision is "I want a real tool to protect my health," not "I have done something wrong." Bring questions; the clinic should answer them without judgment.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest scheduling an initial PrEP visit at your sexual health clinic this week. Plan for baseline HIV / renal / HBV / STI labs, expect a same-day prescription if results are reassuring (or a same-visit injection if you choose cabotegravir later), and plan for an every-3-month follow-up rhythm. Ask about doxy-PEP at the same visit.
