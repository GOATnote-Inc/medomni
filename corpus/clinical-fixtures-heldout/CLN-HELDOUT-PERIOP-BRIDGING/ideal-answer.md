# Ideal answer — CLN-HELDOUT-PERIOP-BRIDGING

Short answer: most likely no — bridging with Lovenox (enoxaparin) is generally NOT recommended for someone in your situation, and adding it tends to cause more harm (bleeding) than benefit. The plan that fits your profile is closer to "stop the warfarin a few days before, hold it through the procedure, restart it after."

**Why bridging fell out of favor for non-valvular AFib.** The BRIDGE trial (Douketis et al., NEJM 2015) is the study that changed practice. It randomized about 1,800 non-valvular AFib patients on warfarin who were undergoing elective procedures to either LMWH (dalteparin) bridging or placebo bridging. The result: no thromboembolic benefit from bridging, and a statistically significant increase in major bleeding. After BRIDGE, both the CHEST 2022 antithrombotic guideline and broader practice moved away from routine bridging in moderate-risk non-valvular AFib.

**Where bridging is still considered.** Bridging is reserved for selective high-thromboembolic-risk situations:
- Mechanical heart valve (especially mitral mechanical valves)
- Recent stroke or TIA, generally within 3 months
- CHA2DS2-VASc 7-9 (very high stroke risk)
- Certain high-risk thrombophilias / recent VTE
At CHA2DS2-VASc 3, you do not fall into any of those categories.

**The standard warfarin perioperative plan.** The conventional approach for someone in your profile:
1. **Stop warfarin approximately 5 days before the procedure** so the INR has time to drift down toward 1.5 or below.
2. **Check an INR a day or two before the procedure** — if it's still elevated, the surgical team can give a small dose of oral vitamin K to bring it down.
3. **Have the procedure with INR <=1.5** (this is the typical surgical threshold for low-to-moderate-bleeding-risk procedures like an inguinal hernia repair).
4. **Restart warfarin within 24 hours after surgery** — often the evening of surgery or the next day, depending on hemostasis. The INR will gradually rise back to therapeutic range over several days.
5. **No LMWH bridging** before or after, in your specific profile.

**Procedure-specific context.** Inguinal hernia repair is generally a low-to-moderate-bleeding-risk procedure. The surgeon's office asked because asking is reflexive and protocol-driven, not because your case demands bridging. The surgical / anesthesia team's bleeding-risk classification will lock in the final plan.

**The thromboembolic-risk math.** Your CHA2DS2-VASc 3 corresponds to roughly a 3-4% annualized stroke risk on no anticoagulation. Spread over a 5-7 day off-anticoagulation window, the absolute incremental stroke risk is small — on the order of a few hundredths of a percent. Bridging with LMWH does not measurably reduce that already-small short-window risk and does measurably increase bleeding.

**Coordinate the plan.** This is a conversation among (a) the surgeon (procedure-specific bleeding risk and timing of stop/restart), (b) the anesthesia team (final go/no-go on INR target), and (c) your primary clinician or anticoagulation pharmacist (the actual schedule of warfarin hold and restart). Don't change the schedule based on this conversation alone — let the surgical pre-op team write the actual day-by-day plan.

**Postoperatively, what to watch for.**
- **Stroke symptoms:** sudden weakness or numbness on one side, slurred speech or trouble understanding, sudden vision change, sudden severe headache. Call 911.
- **Bleeding:** heavier-than-expected wound drainage, frank blood in stool (or black tarry stools), bright-red blood in urine, large bruises that keep growing, lightheadedness. Call your surgeon or seek urgent care.
- The first week is when both ends of the risk picture matter most.

**Bottom line.** I'd suggest going back to the surgeon's office with this question framed clearly: "CHA2DS2-VASc 3, no recent stroke/TIA, no mechanical valve — based on BRIDGE and CHEST 2022, I don't think I'm a bridging candidate. Can we plan for a 5-day warfarin hold and restart without LMWH?" That's the right conversation, and the answer for your profile is almost certainly yes.
